# Google Apprenticeship — ApprenticeEdge Prep Pack

## The Programme

**What it is:** Google offers Higher Apprenticeships in the UK across several business functions. Not as widely publicised as the Big Four programmes — fewer places, high competition from a different kind of candidate pool.

**Length:** 13–18 months (Level 4 Higher Apprenticeship)

**Salary:** £25,000–£35,000 starting (London). Google pays competitively.

**Location:** London (Central Saint Giles HQ primarily)

**Roles available (vary by year):**
- Business Operations
- Sales (gTech, Large Customer Sales)
- Marketing
- People Operations (HR)
- Finance
- Technical (IT, Infrastructure support)

**Qualification:** Level 4 Higher Apprenticeship in relevant area. Not a degree.

**Application window:** Varies — Google doesn't follow a fixed academic calendar. Watch Google Careers and RateMyApprenticeship. Applications often open January–March for September starts.

## The Application Stages

### Stage 1 — Online Application
CV and cover letter. Google reads cover letters carefully — this is not a firm that uses automated screening only.

**Google's question:** Effectively "why Google and why this role?" Google wants curiosity and passion for technology, not just a desire to work at a prestigious company.

**Tip:** Name a specific Google product, initiative, or business area. Show you use and think about Google's products.

### Stage 2 — Online Assessments or Phone Screen
Google varies this by role. Some routes go to a phone screen with a recruiter before assessments. Others use:
- **Cognitive assessments** (SHL-style numerical and logical)
- **Work sample tasks** — e.g. for Sales roles, a mock outreach email; for Ops roles, a data task in Sheets

**Tip:** If you get a recruiter call — be curious. Ask a smart question about the team. Recruiters at Google have genuine influence over whether you progress.

### Stage 3 — Interviews (1–3 rounds)
Google uses a structured interview process. Depending on the role:

- **Behavioural interview** — STAR format, but Google also asks "Googleyness" questions (see below)
- **Technical screen** (for technical roles) — basic problem solving, may involve spreadsheets or SQL
- **Case-style interview** (for Sales/Ops) — "How would you approach X problem?"

**Typical questions:**
- Tell me about a time you had to figure something out without any guidance.
- Describe a project you're proud of. What would you do differently?
- Tell me about a time you worked with a team that had conflicting priorities.
- What is a Google product you think could be improved, and how?
- Describe a situation where you used data to make a decision.

**"Googleyness" questions — what they're really testing:**
Google wants people who are: intellectually humble, curious, collaborative, and comfortable with ambiguity. They call this "Googleyness." Questions probe:
- Do you help others without being asked?
- Are you comfortable not knowing the answer and figuring it out?
- Do you have a genuine point of view, not just a desire to please?

### Stage 4 — Final Interview / Panel
For apprentice roles this is usually one or two final interviews with the hiring manager and a team member. Conversational but still structured.

## Commercial Awareness — What You Must Know About Google

### History in Brief

Larry Page and Sergey Brin met as PhD students at Stanford University and began developing a search algorithm called PageRank — which ranked web pages by the number and quality of links pointing to them. They incorporated Google in September 1998, working initially out of a garage in Menlo Park, California. The name derived from "googol," the mathematical term for 1 followed by 100 zeros. The company's IPO in 2004 at $85 per share made Page and Brin billionaires and set the template for Silicon Valley IPOs that followed.

Eric Schmidt served as CEO from 2001 to 2011, providing the management structure that allowed Page and Brin to focus on product. Larry Page returned as CEO before handing the role to Sundar Pichai in 2015 — the same year Google restructured under a new parent company, Alphabet, to separate its core advertising business from its moonshot bets (Waymo, DeepMind, Verily). Pichai became CEO of Alphabet in 2019. Today Alphabet is one of the most valuable companies in the world, with Google Search at its core.

**Revenue (FY2024):** ~$350bn (Alphabet, Google's parent). Google is one of the most profitable companies ever built.

**What Google does:**
- **Search:** ~$175bn/year in ad revenue. The core engine of everything.
- **YouTube:** Second largest search engine in the world. $35bn+ ad revenue.
- **Google Cloud:** Fast-growing cloud platform, competing with AWS and Azure. ~$40bn revenue.
- **Android:** Powers 72% of smartphones globally.
- **Google Workspace:** Gmail, Docs, Drive, Meet — enterprise productivity.
- **Waymo, DeepMind, Verily:** Moonshot bets (autonomous vehicles, AI research, life sciences).
- **Google Ads / gTech:** The sales and technology arm that helps businesses advertise.

**Current Google talking points (2025–26):**
- Gemini AI and its integration across all Google products
- Antitrust cases in US and EU — Google's dominance of search under scrutiny
- Google's response to ChatGPT and AI search (Perplexity, OpenAI search)
- Google Cloud growth vs. AWS/Azure competition
- YouTube's growing role as a TV replacement for under-35s

## Why Google Over Other Tech Companies (Amazon, Meta, etc.)

**Example:** *"What draws me to Google specifically is the breadth of the business — Search, Cloud, YouTube, hardware, health — all under one roof. In a [Sales/Operations/Finance] role, I'd be exposed to a portfolio of products that no other company can match. I'm also drawn to how Google approaches data and decisions — I want to work somewhere where analytical thinking is the default, not the exception."*
