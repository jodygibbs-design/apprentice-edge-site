# EY School Leaver Programme — ApprenticeEdge Prep Pack

## The Programme

EY's School Leaver Programme is a higher apprenticeship combining professional qualifications with genuine early client contact. EY publishes its salary bands openly and puts more emphasis on strengths than any other Big Four firm — which changes how you need to prepare.

**Length:** 3–4 years to chartered qualification

**Salary:** £24,000–£28,000 starting (London higher). EY publishes salary bands openly on their website.

**Locations:** London (multiple offices), Birmingham, Bristol, Cambridge, Edinburgh, Leeds, Manchester, Reading

**Streams available:**
- Assurance (Audit)
- Tax
- Strategy and Transactions
- Consulting
- Technology
- Law (EY Law — relatively unique among Big Four)

**Qualification:** ACA for Assurance and Tax, or relevant professional qualification for other streams. All fully funded.

**Application window:** Opens September/October. EY recruits on a rolling basis — early application significantly increases your chances of progressing before cohort sizes fill.

## The Application Stages

### Stage 1 — Online Application

Standard form with a service line selection. Research your chosen service line before you commit to it in the application — EY assess whether your stated reasons and your background are coherent.

**EY's values:** integrity, respect, teaming, inclusiveness, energy, enthusiasm. Know them. EY places more emphasis on "teaming" than the other Big Four — it reflects a genuine cultural priority, and it will show up in every stage.

### Stage 2 — EY Online Assessments

EY's assessment suite is different from standard Big Four psychometric tests, and most candidates don't prepare specifically for it.

- **EY Strengths Assessment:** This is not a pass/fail test — EY uses it to match you to teams and understand your profile. The critical instruction is to answer honestly. Candidates who try to game it by selecting what they think EY wants end up with an incoherent profile that flags during the human review. Know your genuine top three strengths before you start and let those come through.
- **Numerical Reasoning:** Data tables, graphs, percentages. Standard timed format.
- **Situational Judgement Test:** Professional scenarios. Values alignment matters more than speed.

### Stage 3 — Video Interview (HireVue)

5–6 questions, 30 seconds preparation, 2-minute response window.

**Typical questions:**
- What does exceptional client service look like to you?
- Tell me about a time you worked with someone very different from yourself.
- Describe a situation where you had to learn something quickly.
- Why EY specifically — what is it about our approach that resonates with you?
- Tell me about a time you showed resilience when things didn't go to plan.

Prepare a specific, genuine answer to the "why EY" question. The strengths-based culture means they're looking for evidence that EY's approach genuinely fits how you work — not that you've read their website.

### Stage 4 — Assessment Centre (EY's Final Stage Assessment)

Usually a half day. Structure varies by service line.

**What happens:**
1. **Group exercise** — collaborative case study with 5–8 candidates. EY specifically values quality of contribution over quantity. Two sharp, well-reasoned points score higher than five weak ones. Show that you listen before you speak.
2. **Individual case study presentation** — given material 20–30 minutes beforehand. Present a recommendation, then take questions. EY assessors ask follow-up questions — they want to see what's behind your answer, not just the answer.
3. **Partner interview** — 45 minutes. Begins with "tell me about yourself" as a narrative opener, then probes with competency questions. EY partners ask follow-ups — prepare for depth, not breadth.

**What EY specifically penalises:**
- Dominating the group exercise rather than amplifying others. "Teaming" is the value EY cares about most — demonstrate it actively.
- Shallow answers in the partner interview. They will follow up on anything that sounds rehearsed.
- Generic "why EY" answers. If you couldn't swap your answer to describe Deloitte, it's not good enough.

## EY Core Competencies

| Competency | What EY actually tests |
|---|---|
| Client focus | Do you understand what the client needs, or just what you think they need? Show you've considered the other perspective first. |
| Teaming | EY is serious about this. Do you build trust, contribute fairly, and amplify others? Stories where you made the team better. |
| Learning agility | Can you pick up new skills and apply them fast? Specific examples of learning under time pressure. |
| Integrity | Honest and ethical under pressure — not just when it's easy. Real stakes. |
| Commercial insight | Understanding how businesses operate and create value — what are the levers? What does a client actually care about? |
| Communication | Adjusting style to your audience and listening well. "I adapted my approach" needs to be specific about how. |

## Commercial Awareness — What You Must Know About EY

**Revenue (FY2024):** $51bn globally. Typically second or third largest of the Big Four.

**What EY does:**
- **Assurance:** Audit of financial statements. EY has a strong financial services practice — audits major banks and insurers.
- **Tax:** Corporate tax, indirect taxes, international tax, private client.
- **Strategy and Transactions (SaT):** M&A advisory, valuations, integration. Formerly Transaction Advisory Services.
- **Consulting:** Business transformation, supply chain, people advisory.
- **Technology:** EY Tech Hub, digital transformation, cybersecurity.
- **EY Law:** Legal services. This is relatively unique among the Big Four and signals EY's ambition to be a multidisciplinary firm.

**The story you must know:** EY's "Project Everest" — the attempted split of the audit and consulting businesses, blocked by partners in 2023 after a long internal debate. This is the single most important thing happening in EY's recent history. If you're asked about challenges facing professional services firms, reference it. It demonstrates you actually follow the industry, not just that you've googled "what does EY do."

**Current EY talking points — know at least two:**
- The aftermath of Project Everest and what it means for Big Four structure
- EY Atlas — AI tools for audit workflow
- Growth in financial services audit
- ESG and sustainability reporting assurance

## Why EY Over the Other Big Four

**What assessors are really asking:** Why the strengths-based approach over a more traditional competency framework, and what specifically about EY's position in your chosen service line fits your goals?

**Example:** *"EY's emphasis on strengths-based development — investing in what people naturally do well rather than trying to make everyone the same — reflects the kind of environment I want to work in. The EY Strengths Assessment itself signals a culture that takes individual development seriously. I'm specifically drawn to [Assurance/SaT/Technology] because [genuine specific reason], and EY's strength in [financial services clients / transactions / tech delivery] means I'd be working on the most complex work in that area from early on."*

## Checklist — Before You Submit

- [ ] Know EY's six values and which ones genuinely match your strengths — the Strengths Assessment will be more coherent if you're already clear on this
- [ ] Understand what ACA means and why it matters (ICAEW website — 15 minutes)
- [ ] Know the Project Everest story at a high level — what it was, why it failed, what it reveals about Big Four governance
- [ ] Five STAR stories plus three strengths-based answers — "when do you feel most energised?" requires genuine self-knowledge
- [ ] Research your chosen service line on EY's careers site
- [ ] Prepare a smart, specific question for your partner interviewer — not something answerable with a Google search
- [ ] A credible "why EY not PwC/Deloitte/KPMG" answer that names something specific
