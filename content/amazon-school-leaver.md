# Amazon Apprenticeship — ApprenticeEdge Prep Pack

## The Programme

Amazon runs multiple apprenticeship programmes across the UK, from Level 3 through to Level 6 degree apprenticeships. The corporate programmes — Tech, Finance, Operations — are competitive. What makes Amazon different from every other employer in this guide is the Leadership Principles. These are not HR window dressing. Every single question in every stage of the process maps to one of the 16 Leadership Principles, and they will know if you haven't prepared them.

**Length:** 15 months (Level 4) to 4 years (Level 6 degree)

**Salary:** £24,000–£32,000 starting. Some apprentice cohorts also receive RSU stock grants.

**Locations:** London (corporate roles), Manchester, Edinburgh, plus fulfilment centres nationally

**Programmes available:**
- Software Development Engineer Apprenticeship (Level 6)
- IT Operations Technician (Level 3/4)
- Finance Apprenticeship
- HR Apprenticeship
- Marketing/Communications
- Operations (Fulfilment/Logistics management)

**Application window:** Amazon recruits year-round on a rolling basis, not a fixed academic calendar. Check Amazon Jobs UK directly and apply as soon as a relevant role opens.

## The Application Stages

### Stage 1 — Online Application

CV and application form. Amazon's ATS screens for keywords from the job description — your CV needs to contain the language Amazon uses, not just describe what you've done in your own words.

**The Amazon-specific requirement:** Some applications include questions framed directly around Amazon's Leadership Principles. Before you write anything, read all 16 LPs and know them by name and meaning. This is not optional preparation — it's the baseline for the entire process.

### Stage 2 — Online Assessments

- **Work Style Assessment:** A personality and values alignment tool. Answer as you would actually behave, not as you think Amazon wants you to behave. The assessment is designed to detect inconsistency — trying to optimise your answers creates an incoherent profile that gets flagged.
- **Technical Assessment** (for tech roles): Coding problems in your language of choice. LeetCode easy to medium difficulty. Python is the safest choice — Amazon uses it heavily.

### Stage 3 — Phone or Video Screen

Usually with a recruiter or hiring manager. 30–45 minutes.

**Typical questions:**
- Walk me through a project or achievement you're proud of.
- Tell me about a time you had to deal with a difficult person.
- Describe a time you made a mistake. What did you learn?
- Tell me about a time you did more than was required.

**The thing every question is doing:** Every question maps to a specific Leadership Principle. "Tell me about a difficult person" = Earn Trust. "Did more than required" = Bias for Action. "Made a mistake" = Learn and Be Curious. Know which LP you're demonstrating before you answer. If you can't identify the LP, you haven't prepared enough.

### Stage 4 — The Loop

Amazon calls its final stage the "Loop" — typically three to five back-to-back 1:1 interviews with different members of the team. One of those interviewers is the "Bar Raiser."

**The Bar Raiser:** An independent Amazon employee, from outside your hiring team, whose sole job is to maintain hiring standards. They have veto power over the hire. The Bar Raiser's questions are harder and more abstract. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with your manager and turned out to be right" is a typical Bar Raiser question. Prepare specifically for this — it tests whether you have genuine backbone (Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit) without being confrontational.

**What makes the Loop hard:**
- Each interviewer focuses on two to three specific Leadership Principles. The same LP may come up in multiple interviews — you need more than one STAR story per principle.
- Interviewers compare notes and vote independently in a debrief after the Loop. Your consistency and the quality of your specifics across all interviews is what determines the hire decision.
- Vague or unmeasurable outcomes will not score. "It worked out well" is not a result.

## Amazon's 16 Leadership Principles — Know All of These

| # | Principle | What it means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Customer Obsession | Start with what the customer needs, work backwards from there |
| 2 | Ownership | Act like it's your company, not just your job. "That's not my problem" does not exist. |
| 3 | Invent and Simplify | Find new, simpler ways to do things. Complexity is a cost. |
| 4 | Are Right, A Lot | Make sound judgements with incomplete information. |
| 5 | Learn and Be Curious | Never stop learning. Stay intellectually open, especially when you're wrong. |
| 6 | Hire and Develop the Best | Raise the bar. Invest in others' growth. |
| 7 | Insist on the Highest Standards | Don't accept good enough. Errors get traced to root cause. |
| 8 | Think Big | Set bold goals — not just incremental improvements. |
| 9 | Bias for Action | Move fast. Calculated risk is valued. Inaction is not safe by default. |
| 10 | Frugality | Do more with less. Constraints drive creativity. |
| 11 | Earn Trust | Be honest, transparent, self-critical. Especially in public. |
| 12 | Dive Deep | Get into the details. Manage with data, not assumption. |
| 13 | Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit | Challenge respectfully. Once a decision is made, commit fully. |
| 14 | Deliver Results | Ultimately, results matter more than effort. |
| 15 | Strive to be Earth's Best Employer | Amazon as a good place to work. |
| 16 | Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility | The bigger Amazon gets, the more it owes. |

**Prepare at least one STAR story for each of:** Customer Obsession, Ownership, Bias for Action, Earn Trust, Deliver Results, Learn and Be Curious, Have Backbone. These appear most often. Have two stories per principle if you can — the Loop requires it.

## Amazon STAR Stories — What Makes Them Strong

Amazon expects specific, measurable outcomes. Vague results are the single most common reason candidates fail the Loop.

**What a strong result looks like:**
- "Reduced the time to complete the task by 30%"
- "The event had 40% higher attendance than the previous year"
- "I identified an error that would have required the team to resubmit three weeks of work"
- "My suggestion was adopted and we delivered the project two days ahead of schedule"

**What a weak result looks like:**
- "It worked out well in the end"
- "The team was pleased with the outcome"
- "I learned a lot from the experience"

If your result doesn't tell the interviewer what concretely changed because of your action, it won't score. Rewrite every story until the result is specific.

## Commercial Awareness — What You Must Know About Amazon

**Revenue (FY2024):** ~$590bn. Amazon is one of the largest companies in history.

**The revenue split — know this:**
- **E-commerce:** The core retail business. Amazon Marketplace (third-party sellers) is now larger than Amazon's own first-party retail.
- **AWS (Amazon Web Services):** Cloud computing. ~$100bn revenue. The most profitable part of Amazon by a large margin. AWS subsidises everything else. This is the business that matters most financially.
- **Advertising:** $50bn+ revenue. Amazon Ads is the third-largest digital advertising platform after Google and Meta.
- **Prime:** ~200m global subscribers. Bundles delivery, video, music. The loyalty engine.
- **Fulfilment and Logistics:** One of the most sophisticated logistics networks ever built.

**Current Amazon talking points — know at least two:**
- AWS in the GenAI infrastructure race — competing with Google Cloud and Azure for enterprise AI workloads
- Amazon's return-to-office mandate and workforce culture shift
- Regulatory pressure on Amazon's marketplace dominance (EU Digital Markets Act)
- Project Kuiper — Amazon's satellite internet play, competing directly with Starlink
- Amazon's last-mile delivery expansion

## Why Amazon Over Google or Other Tech Companies

**What interviewers are listening for:** That you've chosen Amazon because of the specific scale and complexity of the problems, and because the Leadership Principles genuinely match how you work. Not because Amazon is a big name.

**Example:** *"Amazon's scope across retail, cloud, logistics, and advertising means an apprenticeship here exposes you to problems that don't exist anywhere else. I'm specifically interested in [AWS/Finance/Operations] because [specific reason]. The Leadership Principles also genuinely align with how I approach work — particularly Ownership and Bias for Action. I want to be somewhere that rewards initiative and holds people accountable for outcomes, not just effort."*

## Checklist — Before You Submit

- [ ] Memorise all 16 Leadership Principles — not just the names, but what each means in practice
- [ ] Prepare at least seven STAR stories, each linked to a specific LP with a measurable result
- [ ] Check every STAR result — if it's vague, rewrite it until it's specific
- [ ] Research your specific programme (SDE, Finance, Operations) and know what you'd actually do day-to-day
- [ ] For tech roles: practice LeetCode easy problems in Python. Be comfortable explaining your approach out loud.
- [ ] Know AWS revenue and why it matters to Amazon's overall business model
- [ ] Prepare specifically for the Bar Raiser question type: "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a decision and were right"
