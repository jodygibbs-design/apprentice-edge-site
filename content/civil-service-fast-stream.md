# Civil Service Fast Stream — ApprenticeEdge Prep Pack

## The Programme

The Civil Service Fast Stream is graduate-entry. The school leaver route in is the **Civil Service Higher Apprenticeship** — a direct path into the same career ladder without a degree. High-performing apprenticeship completers can apply directly to the Fast Stream afterwards. The programmes are structured, transparent in progression, and offer scale of impact that no private sector role at this level comes close to.

**Length:** 2 years (Higher Apprenticeship, Level 4)

**Salary:** £27,000–£30,000 in London; £24,000–£26,000 nationally

**Location:** London for most departments, with regional postings across the UK

**Streams available at school leaver/apprentice level:**
- Government Communications Service Apprenticeship
- Digital, Data and Technology (DDaT) Fast Stream Apprenticeship
- HR Apprenticeship
- Finance Apprenticeship
- Project Delivery Apprenticeship

**Application window:** Opens October/November annually. The process takes 4–6 months from application to offer. Don't stop applying elsewhere until your start date is confirmed.

## The Application Stages

### Stage 1 — Online Application

The Civil Service uses the **Success Profiles** framework for all applications. Every question maps to one of five elements:

- **Behaviours** — how you act in work situations
- **Strengths** — what you naturally do well and enjoy
- **Ability** — cognitive aptitude
- **Experience** — your background and track record
- **Technical** — job-specific skills (varies by role)

**At apprentice level, Behaviours and Strengths are weighted most heavily.** Know all 10 Civil Service Behaviours before you write a single word of your application (listed below). The application will tell you which behaviours are being assessed — prepare specific STAR examples for each one named.

### Stage 2 — Online Tests (Civil Service Judgement Test)

The Civil Service uses its own bespoke assessments, not standard SHL or Korn Ferry tests. You cannot usefully practice SHL and expect the same format.

- **Civil Service Judgement Test:** Situational judgement in a government work context. The correct answers reflect Civil Service values — impartiality, integrity, objectivity, honesty, and democratic accountability. Answers that reflect strong partisan instincts or that bypass established process consistently score low.
- **Civil Service Verbal Test:** Reading comprehension and language precision. Accuracy matters more than speed.
- **Numerical Reasoning** — included in some streams.

**One thing that trips people up:** The Judgement Test specifically rewards politically neutral, procedure-respecting answers. Instincts shaped by private sector or business thinking can point you wrong. Read the Civil Service values before you start.

### Stage 3 — Video Interview or Telephone Interview

Structured around Civil Service Behaviours. You will be told in advance which behaviours are being assessed — prepare specific stories for exactly those behaviours, not general prep.

**The ten Civil Service Behaviours:**
1. Making Effective Decisions
2. Leadership
3. Communicating and Influencing
4. Working Together
5. Delivering at Pace
6. Developing Self and Others
7. Managing a Quality Service
8. Changing and Improving
9. Seeing the Big Picture
10. Handling a Changing Environment

**STAR format applies with one important addition:** After the Result, Civil Service interviewers routinely ask "What would you do differently next time?" Prepare this for every single story. It's not an optional follow-up — it's a standard part of the assessment.

### Stage 4 — Assessment Centre

Varies by stream but typically includes:

1. **Behaviour-based interview** — 45 minutes, 3–4 behaviours assessed. Deep questioning — they follow up on answers.
2. **Group exercise** — policy scenario or public service case study. Show structured thinking and collaborative instinct.
3. **Written exercise** — briefing note or policy analysis. This is the most Civil Service-specific task in the process, and most candidates get the format wrong.
4. **Presentation** (some streams)

**On the written exercise:** Civil Service writing has a specific format. A good submission is structured as: **Purpose → Key Points → Recommendation → Risks.** The recommendation goes near the top, not at the end. Concise. No jargon. Written for a minister who has 3 minutes. Practice writing in this format before the AC — you will be at a disadvantage if you arrive without having done it.

## Civil Service Core Competencies

Focus preparation on these five behaviours — they appear in almost every Civil Service interview:

| Behaviour | What assessors look for |
|---|---|
| Making Effective Decisions | You gather evidence, consider options, and make clear decisions with imperfect information — not just after everything is certain. |
| Communicating and Influencing | You tailor your message to the audience, listen actively, and persuade through reasoning — not just assertion. |
| Working Together | You build relationships and collaborate across boundaries. Civil Service work is inherently cross-departmental. |
| Delivering at Pace | You manage competing priorities and deliver on time — with evidence of how you tracked and managed the work. |
| Changing and Improving | You spot what isn't working and act on it — not just flag it. Concrete improvements you've made. |

## Commercial Awareness — The Civil Service Context

The Civil Service isn't a commercial business, but situational awareness still matters. They call it **"Seeing the Big Picture"** — do you understand the context you'd be working in?

**What you need to understand:**
- **How government actually works:** Parliament passes legislation. Ministers set policy direction. Civil servants advise ministers and implement policy. The Civil Service is politically neutral — it serves whichever government is in power, regardless of party. You cannot be partisan in any interview answer.
- **Key departments:** HMRC, Home Office, DWP, DHSC, MOD, DCMS, FCDO, HM Treasury, Cabinet Office — know what each does at a high level. Know which department your chosen stream sits within.
- **Current government priorities (2025–26):** Economic growth, NHS reform, planning reform, AI regulation, net zero, defence spending. The King's Speech and GOV.UK news are the right sources.
- **Civil Service reform:** The ongoing push to digitalise public services. The DDaT stream is expanding rapidly.

**The four Civil Service values — memorise these:**
- Integrity
- Honesty
- Objectivity
- Impartiality

Every answer you give should be consistent with these values. An answer that implies personal political opinion — even a mild one — signals you don't understand what it means to be a civil servant.

## Why the Civil Service Over Private Sector

This question distinguishes candidates who've made a deliberate choice from those treating the Civil Service as a backup. The interview expects a genuine answer.

**Example:** *"I want work that has impact at scale — decisions made in government affect millions of people, and that scale of consequence isn't available in any private sector role at this stage of a career. I'm specifically drawn to [DDaT/Project Delivery/Finance] because [genuine specific reason]. The apprenticeship to Fast Stream pathway is also a structured, transparent career route — the criteria for progression are explicit in a way that private sector paths often aren't."*
